AI & Technology
Compliance Calendar

The federal calendar tells you when the law requires action. This calendar tells you when your AI and technology decisions create compliance exposure, before a reviewer finds the gap. No regulatory deadlines are invented. Every "recommended window" is labeled as practice, not statute.

Version: Spring 2026  ·  For practitioners and policy students

How this calendar works. Federal program deadlines are set by statute and regulation. AI and technology compliance obligations are mostly documentation-driven — they don't have a single annual filing date. What they have are trigger events: a tool contract that expires, a DPA that wasn't renewed, a staff training cycle that wasn't documented, a board approval that was never recorded. This calendar maps when those triggers typically hit, what the documentation gap looks like to a reviewer, and what to do about it. The "Hard Deadline" entries are genuine fixed dates. Everything else is a recommended window based on when the risk typically surfaces.

Decision window — act before it closes

Decision window means there is a period of time during which a district must act to avoid a compliance gap. Unlike a hard deadline, there is no single regulatory filing date — but if the window closes without action, the district will enter the next phase (audit season, new school year, or contract renewal) without the required documentation in place.

Q1 — January through March
Decision Window
Mid-Year AI Tool Inventory Review
January 6 – February 28, 2026 · No regulatory filing date

January is when tool sprawl from the prior semester becomes visible. Teachers have adopted tools informally since August, and the gap between what's on your approved list and what's actually running in classrooms is now measurable. A mid-year inventory is the last realistic opportunity to pull unauthorized tools before they appear in a student data audit. What a reviewer asks: Did you know what tools were in use? Did you have signed DPAs for all of them?

What to complete in this window
  • Pull a complete inventory of AI and edtech tools in active use, both approved and unapproved
  • Flag any tool accessing student data without a signed DPA on file
  • Verify board approval is documented for each AI tool adoption since July
  • Remove or restrict any tool that cannot produce a DPA path within 30 days
  • Update the approved tool list. Remove tools with defunct vendors
Basis: FERPA data governance requirements (20 U.S.C. §1232g) · CoSN Privacy Framework · Student Data Privacy Consortium NDPA standards
Internal Action
AI Incident Response Plan: Tabletop Review
February 1 – March 13, 2026 · Not a regulatory deadline

Most districts have a general cybersecurity incident response plan. Fewer have one that covers AI-specific incidents: an AI tool generating harmful content, a vendor breach exposing student data ingested by an AI system, or a generative AI output used to make a consequential decision about a student. The PowerSchool breach (December 2024) demonstrated that AI-adjacent systems are now breach vectors. February is low-stakes; run a 90-minute tabletop before you need it for real.

What to review and document
  • Confirm your existing incident response plan names AI tools explicitly
  • Identify who is responsible for AI-specific incidents (tech director, privacy officer, or superintendent)
  • Document the notification protocol if a student-facing AI tool generates harmful output
  • Confirm your vendor DPAs include breach notification timelines
  • Record the tabletop session: date, participants, outcomes, and any plan amendments
Basis: ED Student Privacy Policy Office guidance · CISA K-12 cybersecurity recommendations · State data breach notification laws (vary by state) · FERPA breach response obligations
Hard Deadline
COPPA Compliance Review: Student-Facing Tools
April 22, 2026 · Amended COPPA Rule operator compliance deadline (16 CFR Part 312) · Ongoing district obligation triggered by each new tool deployment for students under 13

COPPA (15 U.S.C. §§6501–6506) requires verifiable parental consent before operators collect personal information from children under 13. In school settings, a district may authorize a vendor’s data collection on behalf of parents for tools used in the classroom, but this authorization does not eliminate the district’s responsibility to confirm the vendor’s COPPA compliance path and to disclose the tool’s data practices to parents. This is not an annual filing. It is triggered every time a new tool is deployed to students under 13. Q1 is when to confirm that every tool adopted since August has a documented compliance path on file.

What to verify for each student-facing tool
  • Confirm the tool's COPPA compliance method: school-authorized consent, direct parental consent, or age-gate that excludes students under 13
  • If school-authorized: confirm the vendor's privacy policy and DPA describe the school authorization mechanism and restrict the vendor's use of collected data to educational purposes
  • Confirm parent notification language covers the tool's data collection in your annual FERPA/COPPA rights notice
  • Document the age range of students using each tool. No student under 13 should be on a tool without a documented compliance path.
Source: FTC COPPA Rule, ftc.gov · 15 U.S.C. §§6501–6506 · FTC COPPA Rule: 16 CFR Part 312 · FERPA school official exception: 34 CFR §99.31(a)(1)(i)
Q2 — April through June
Decision Window
Next-Year AI Tool Adoption Decisions: Procurement Window
April 1 – May 30, 2026 · Board approval and DPA execution must precede July 2026 deployment

Districts that want to deploy new AI tools at the start of next school year must begin procurement in Q2. A tool approved by the board in September after students are already using it is a retroactive approval, which is exactly what a compliance reviewer flags. Board approval, DPA execution, and staff training documentation all take time. April is the last realistic start date for a September-ready tool. What a reviewer asks: Was the tool vetted before it was used? When was the DPA signed? When did the board approve it?

What must happen before July deployment
  • Tool vetting completed and documented: written decision to adopt, restrict, or reject with supporting evidence on file
  • DPA executed with vendor: signed, dated, and on file before any student data is shared
  • Board agenda item prepared: adoption memo, privacy review summary, and cost/benefit documented
  • Staff training plan drafted. Initial training must precede student access.
  • Output review policy written if the tool generates student-facing AI content
Basis: FERPA §99.31(a)(1) school official exception conditions · CoSN K-12 Cybersecurity and Privacy Framework · Student Data Privacy Consortium NDPA
Internal Action
Staff AI Training Documentation Audit
May 1 – June 12, 2026 · Complete before last day of instruction

If your district has deployed AI tools in classrooms, you have a training documentation obligation, not always by statute but by practice. When an AI tool incident occurs and a reviewer asks "were staff trained on appropriate use?", the answer must be a record, not a memory. May is when to confirm that every tool deployment this year has associated training documentation before the school year ends and records become harder to reconstruct.

What to document before June 12, 2026
  • Training completion records for every AI tool deployed to staff: dates, participants, and format
  • Training content on file: what was covered, who delivered it, and what the guardrails were
  • Acceptable Use Policy updated to reference AI tools specifically (not just "the internet")
  • Student AI use policy in effect, documented in student handbook or equivalent
  • Any disciplinary or misuse incidents documented with resolution. No undocumented incidents.
Basis: FERPA school official exception conditions (34 CFR §99.31(a)(1)) — districts must maintain direct control over vendor data practices · District AUP obligations under CIPA (47 U.S.C. §254(h)) · CoSN K-12 Privacy Framework
Hard Deadline
Annual DPA Renewal Review: Contract Expiration Audit
June 30, 2026 · DPAs on school-year terms expire; a lapsed DPA means student data sharing continues without a current agreement

Data Privacy Agreements are contracts. Most are written with a one-year term tied to the academic year. A DPA that expires on June 30 and is not renewed means the district is sharing student data with a vendor without a current agreement in effect. When a DPA lapses, the conditions for the FERPA school official exception (34 CFR §99.31(a)(1)) may no longer be met, creating potential FERPA exposure beginning July 1. This is not a "recommended review." This is a hard contract deadline that most districts do not track systematically. What a reviewer asks: Is the DPA current? Pull it. If the vendor is still active and the DPA expired six months ago, you have a gap.

What to complete before June 30
  • Pull all DPAs currently on file. Confirm the expiration date on each.
  • Identify every DPA expiring between July 1 and December 31 of this calendar year
  • Begin renewal outreach to vendors no later than 60 days before expiration
  • Confirm vendor is still operational. Do not renew a DPA with a defunct or acquired company without reviewing what changed
  • Document any tool you cannot get a current DPA for. That tool cannot continue to access student data
Source: ED Student Privacy Policy Office, studentprivacy.ed.gov · FERPA 34 CFR §99.31(a)(1) school official exception, requires current agreement · Student Data Privacy Consortium NDPA template (annual term standard)
Q3 — July through September
Hard Deadline
New School Year Tool Deployment: Documentation Must Precede Student Access
On or before first day of instruction, 2026–2027 · DPA signature date must precede first student use date. Sequencing is the audit test.

There is no federal statute that says "complete your DPA by August." What exists is a sequencing requirement that functions as a hard deadline: student data cannot be shared with a vendor until a DPA is in place. If your district deploys an AI tool to students on Day 1 of school and the DPA was not signed before Day 1, you have a retroactive documentation gap. This is the most common AI compliance failure pattern. The question a reviewer asks is simple: which came first: the DPA or the student data?

Sequencing is the audit test. If you can produce a DPA with a signature date before the tool's first use date, you are compliant. If the DPA is dated after the tool went live, you have a documentation gap regardless of what the DPA says. Sequence your paperwork before you schedule your training.
What must be complete before student instruction begins
  • DPA signed and dated. The signature date must precede the first student use date for every tool.
  • Board approval on record: adoption documented in board minutes before deployment
  • Output review policy written and distributed to staff for any generative AI tool
  • Staff training completed and documented. A training record must exist before student access.
  • Student and parent notification in place. AI tool use disclosed in handbook or equivalent
Basis: FERPA 34 CFR §99.31(a)(1), school official exception requires prior agreement · CoSN Privacy Framework, deployment sequencing standard
Internal Action
Board AI Policy Review: Annual Update Cycle
August 1 – September 30, 2026 · Align with opening board meeting cycle

Most districts adopted their first AI-related board policy in 2023 or 2024, often reactively and quickly. The AI landscape has changed materially since then: new state guidance in California, the collapse of multiple edtech vendors, generative AI now embedded in core tools like Google Workspace and Microsoft 365, and new federal rulemaking signals. An AI policy that hasn't been reviewed since 2023 likely doesn't cover the tools your district is currently using. What a reviewer asks: Does your board policy reflect your current practice? If not, which one controls?

What to review and update annually
  • AI and technology use policy language: does it name generative AI specifically?
  • Procurement policy: does it require DPA and Workflow Fit review before board approval?
  • Student acceptable use policy: updated to address AI-generated content and academic integrity
  • Staff acceptable use policy: covers AI tools used in administrative and instructional workflows
  • Data governance policy: addresses third-party AI vendor data retention and deletion rights
Basis: CoSN K-12 Cybersecurity and Privacy Framework · CIPA internet safety policy requirements (47 U.S.C. §254(h)) · FERPA annual notification requirements (34 CFR §99.7)
Hard Deadline
ADA Title II Digital Accessibility Compliance: WCAG 2.1 Level AA
April 24, 2026 (districts serving 50,000+ population) · April 26, 2027 (districts serving under 50,000) · DOJ final rule, published April 24, 2024

The Department of Justice's final rule under Title II of the ADA (published April 24, 2024) requires all public school districts to make their websites, mobile apps, and digital content accessible according to WCAG 2.1 Level AA standards. Districts serving populations of 50,000 or more must comply by April 24, 2026. Districts serving under 50,000 have until April 26, 2027. The Title II rule covers content the district controls directly. Separately, Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act requires districts to ensure that students with disabilities have equal access to instruction — which means AI and edtech tools deployed in classrooms must not create barriers for students with disabilities. A student who cannot access an AI tool their classmates use due to a disability may have a cognizable Section 504 claim, regardless of the district's population size.

What to verify and document before your compliance deadline
  • Confirm your compliance deadline: April 24, 2026 (districts serving 50,000+) or April 26, 2027 (districts serving under 50,000). Use your 2020 decennial Census population, or 2022 SAIPE data for independent school districts.
  • Audit your district's own websites, portals, and digital content against WCAG 2.1 Level AA for Title II compliance. For vendor-provided edtech tools, request VPAT documentation and test for Section 504 equal access obligations. Automated scans catch only 30% of issues; manual testing with a screen reader (NVDA or JAWS) is required
  • Request VPAT (Voluntary Product Accessibility Template) from every vendor; document receipt and review
  • Embed WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliance language into all new vendor contracts and procurement RFPs
  • Document alternative access paths for students whose IEP or 504 plan requires accommodation
Basis: ADA Title II final rule (DOJ, published April 24, 2024; 28 CFR Part 35) — governs district websites, apps, and digital content · Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C. §794) — governs equal access to instruction for students with disabilities · WCAG 2.1 Level AA (W3C, 2018) · OCR enforcement authority over K-12 digital accessibility
Q4 — October through December
Decision Window
Vendor Viability Review: End-of-Year Tool Status Check
November 1 – December 19, 2026 · Complete before contract renewal signing

The edtech vendor collapse pattern is not hypothetical. The PowerSchool breach and reported instability among several AI tutoring platforms in 2024 and 2025 have demonstrated that tools districts depend on can fail, get acquired, or become security liabilities mid-year. End-of-year is when to assess whether each AI tool in your inventory should be renewed, requires a replacement DPA due to acquisition, or should be sunset before next year. What a reviewer asks: Did you know your vendor was in financial distress? When did you find out, and what did you do?

What to assess for each vendor before contract renewal
  • Confirm vendor is still independently operational. Check for acquisition announcements.
  • Review any data breach or security incident disclosed by the vendor this year
  • Confirm DPA terms still reflect current data practices. Acquisitions often change data sharing.
  • Review recent industry reporting and vendor communications for security incidents or ownership changes
  • Document the renewal decision: date, rationale, and who approved it
Basis: FERPA 34 CFR §99.31(a)(1), school official exception requires current, valid agreement · Student Data Privacy Consortium NDPA standard, vendor notification requirements on change of control · PowerSchool breach disclosure (PowerSchool, January 2025)
Internal Action
Annual AI Governance Checkup: Full System Review
October 1 – November 20, 2026 · Complete before Q2 procurement decisions begin

The Governance Checkup is the one annual review that touches every layer: tool inventory, DPA status, board policy, staff training documentation, incident response protocol, and accessibility compliance. Q4 is the right time because it follows the first semester of the school year, which is enough time to see how this year's tools are actually being used, and precedes the Q2 procurement window when next-year adoption decisions begin. A documented Governance Checkup is also the artifact you produce when a reviewer asks "what does your AI governance process look like?"

What the Governance Checkup produces
  • Scored assessment across all five governance domains: Reviewed, Verified, and Leading per domain
  • Findings memo with specific documentation gaps, prioritized by reviewer risk
  • Remediation list: what to fix before Q2 procurement decisions and before any audit
  • Evidence of a documented governance process: the artifact a reviewer accepts as proof of due diligence
Basis: FERPA data governance requirements · CoSN K-12 Privacy Framework
