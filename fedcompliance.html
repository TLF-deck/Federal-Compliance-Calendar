Q1: January through March
Internal Action
Title I Part A: Mid-Year Expenditure Pace Review
Recommended: February through March. Not a federal filing date.

ESSA §1127(a) limits Title I Part A carryover to 15% of a district’s annual allocation. The carryover calculation is made at the close of the federal fiscal year (September 30), not at the end of the school year. Note: the 15% limitation does not apply if the LEA’s total allocation (including any transferred funds) is less than $50,000 for the fiscal year (ESSA §1127(a)). A pace problem identified in February can still be corrected. One identified in August usually cannot. This is a recommended internal review, not a federal filing requirement.

What to review internally
  • Pull current expenditure report against your total Title I Part A allocation
  • Project forward to September 30: will unspent funds exceed 15%?
  • Identify any delayed hires, postponed purchases, or unexpended reserves
  • Document a corrective plan if pace is behind (fund redistribution, supplemental purchases)
Source: ESSA §1127(a) · ESSA full text, ed.gov · Hard federal date is September 30 FY close. This entry is a mid-year precaution.
Internal Action
ESSA Consolidated State Plan: Local Program Alignment Review
Recommended: January through February. No federal filing date for districts.

Each state submits a Consolidated State Plan to ED under ESSA that governs how Title I, II, III, and IV-A funds flow to districts. Districts are bound by their state’s approved plan, but most district administrators have never read it. Q1 is a practical time to verify that local program descriptions and use-of-funds plans remain consistent with what the state certified to ED. Misalignment discovered during a monitoring visit, rather than by the district, is a compliance finding.

What to review internally
  • Locate your state’s current ESSA Consolidated State Plan (available on your SEA’s website)
  • Review Title I supplement-not-supplant documentation against your local budget
  • Confirm that funded Title II professional development activities match plan descriptions
  • Check Title IV-A well-rounded education spending meets the 20% threshold requirement
Source: ESSA, ed.gov · State plans publicly available through your SEA’s website
Q2: April through June
Hard Deadline
FERPA & PPRA Compliance Certification
April 30 deadline established for 2025. Confirm with your SEA each spring whether a new request has been issued.

On March 28, 2025, ED’s Student Privacy Policy Office issued a Dear Colleague Letter alongside its annual FERPA/PPRA notification (required by 20 U.S.C. §1232h(c)(5)(C)), requiring each SEA to submit documentation by April 30, 2025 confirming that the SEA and its LEAs are complying with FERPA and PPRA. As of the Spring 2026 audit of this calendar (March 22, 2026), no equivalent Dear Colleague Letter or April 30 deadline has been issued for 2026. The underlying FERPA and PPRA compliance obligations are standing and year-round regardless of whether a new documentation request is issued. Confirm with your SEA each spring whether a new request has been issued and what district-level response is required.

What to have ready
  • FERPA annual notification: distributed, translated, and documented
  • Directory information policy current and board-approved
  • Records access request procedure documented and accessible to staff
  • PPRA survey notification procedures in place for any third-party surveys
  • Any “gender plan” or similar student-specific documentation reviewed for parental access compliance
  • Confirm with your SEA what district-level documentation they require this spring
Source: ED Student Privacy Policy Office, studentprivacy.ed.gov · FERPA: 20 U.S.C. §1232g; 34 CFR Part 99 · PPRA: 20 U.S.C. §1232h · Dear Colleague Letter issued March 28, 2025. April 30 was a 2025-specific date. No equivalent 2026 letter confirmed as of March 22, 2026.
Internal Action
FERPA Annual Notification: Review and Distribute
Recommended: May through June. No single federal filing date.

34 CFR §99.7 requires LEAs to notify parents and eligible students of their FERPA rights annually. There is no prescribed federal date. The notification must be provided by any means reasonably likely to inform families, and must include provisions for families with disabilities or whose primary language is not English. End-of-year review ensures the notification is accurate and the distribution is documented before the new school year begins.

What to review and document
  • Is the notification content current? (rights to inspect, amend, consent to disclosure, file complaint)
  • Does it include the district’s definition of “school official” and “legitimate educational interest”?
  • Distribution method documented: how was it sent, to whom, and when?
  • Available in all required home languages for your student population?
  • Directory information opt-out process documented and accessible
Source: FERPA, studentprivacy.ed.gov · 34 CFR §99.7 · Annual standing obligation; no single federal filing date
Check Your State
McKinney-Vento: Homeless Student Data Submission
SEA sets the district window, typically spring. Verify annually with your SEA.
Why “check your state”: McKinney-Vento data (EDFacts file specs FS 118, FS 170, FS 194) flows from district to SEA to ED via the EDPass system. The SEA sets the district-level data collection window. FS 170 for SY 2024–25, for example, was due to ED by January 2026, but the district-to-SEA window that feeds it is set by each state individually. The federal obligation is national; the submission calendar is not.

The McKinney-Vento Homeless Assistance Act (Title VII-B, 42 U.S.C. §11431–11435) requires annual data collection on homeless student identification, enrollment, transportation, and services. What a federal or state reviewer asks is whether every identified student has a complete record: dates of identification, services offered, barriers removed, and school-of-origin decisions documented. That question is constant regardless of when your state’s window opens.

What to have ready before your SEA’s window opens
  • Identification logs: date identified, by whom, definition of homelessness applied
  • Enrollment records: immediate enrollment documented, any barriers and how they were removed
  • Transportation records for each student exercising school-of-origin rights
  • Unaccompanied youth records and FAFSA status notifications (if applicable)
  • Services-offered documentation for every identified student this year
  • Liaison annual professional development participation documented (42 U.S.C. §11432(f)(6) & (g)(1)(J)(iv))
Find your SEA’s window: NCHE, nche.ed.gov · Source: McKinney-Vento Act, Title VII-B; EDFacts FS 118, 170, 194 · Liaison professional development: 42 U.S.C. §11432(f)(6) & (g)(1)(J)(iv)
Internal Action
Title III EL Program: Annual Outcome Review
Recommended: May through June. Federal accountability cycle begins here.

ESSA §3121 requires Title III subgrantees to annually report EL student progress toward English proficiency and academic achievement. SEAs use this data to determine whether districts have met Title III accountability targets. Districts that fail to meet targets for two consecutive years face mandatory improvement plans. Q2 is when to review current-year outcome data, before the SEA’s reporting window opens, so that findings do not arrive as surprises during monitoring.

What to review before the SEA reporting window
  • EL student progress on English language proficiency assessments (ELPA) year-over-year
  • Percentage of ELs reclassified as proficient: is the trend moving in the right direction?
  • Title III-funded activity documentation: do records show a clear link between spending and outcomes?
  • Parent notification: were parents of EL students notified within 30 days of placement (ESSA §1112(e)(3)(A))?
Source: ED Title III Program, ed.gov · ESSA §3121 (accountability) · ESSA §1112(e)(3)(A) (parent notification)
Q3: July through September
Hard Deadline
Title I Part A: 15% Carryover Limit
September 30, federal fiscal year close (ESSA §1127(a))

ESSA §1127(a) prohibits districts from carrying over more than 15% of their Title I Part A allocation from one fiscal year to the next. The calculation is made at the close of the federal fiscal year (September 30). Note: the 15% limitation does not apply if the LEA’s total Title I Part A allocation (including any transferred funds) is less than $50,000 for the fiscal year (ESSA §1127(a)). Districts that exceed the 15% limit must document why and request a waiver from their SEA. Repeated carryover can trigger compliance monitoring. The mid-year pace review (see Q1 entry) exists specifically to prevent a problem at this date.

What to have documented by September 30
  • Final expenditure report against total Title I Part A allocation
  • Carryover calculation: confirm at or below 15% of annual allocation
  • If above 15%: written justification drafted and submitted to your SEA
  • Waiver documentation on file if SEA approval was obtained
Source: ESSA §1127(a) · ED Title I Program, ed.gov · This is the actual hard date, not a spring deadline
Check Your State
IDEA Part B: Annual Performance Data Collection
SEA sets district data submission windows, feeding the state SPP/APR cycle
Why “check your state”: IDEA Part B annual performance data flows district → SEA → ED through each state’s State Performance Plan and Annual Performance Report (SPP/APR) process. The SEA sets district-level data submission windows; there is no federal date that applies to districts directly. What is federal and uniform is what the data must show.

IDEA Part B (20 U.S.C. §1412) requires annual data collection across 17 compliance and results indicators including IEP timelines, least restrictive environment (LRE) placements, transition services, and disproportionality. A federal reviewer’s core questions: Were IEP evaluation timelines met? Are LRE placement decisions documented with individualized rationale? Are transition plans in place for all required students?

What to have ready
  • IEP evaluation completion rate: initial evaluations completed within 60 days of consent (34 CFR §300.301(c)(1))
  • LRE placement data: documented rationale for each placement decision. Generic language is a finding.
  • Transition plan documentation: in place for all students no later than the IEP in effect when they turn 16 (federal floor, 34 CFR §300.320(b)). Many states require earlier; confirm your state’s requirement.
  • Disproportionality analysis completed. Significant disproportionality triggers corrective action.
  • Prior Written Notice (PWN) on file for all IEP decisions made this year
Find your SEA’s window: IDEA, sites.ed.gov/idea · 20 U.S.C. §1412 · SPP/APR indicators: 34 CFR §300.600–300.602
Internal Action
Title IV-A SSAE: Spending Threshold Verification
Recommended: August through September, before fiscal year close

ESSA §4105(b) requires districts receiving more than $30,000 in Title IV-A (Student Support and Academic Enrichment) funds to spend at least 20% on well-rounded educational opportunities, at least 20% on safe and healthy students activities, and no more than 15% on technology infrastructure. Districts that do not meet these thresholds can face findings during monitoring. September is the last opportunity to verify allocation percentages before fiscal year close.

What to verify before September 30
  • Well-rounded education activities: at or above 20% of Title IV-A expenditures
  • Safe and healthy students activities: at or above 20% of Title IV-A expenditures
  • Technology infrastructure: at or below 15% of Title IV-A expenditures
  • Activity documentation links each expenditure to an allowable use category
Source: ESSA §4105(b) · ED Title IV-A Program, ed.gov
Q4: October through December
Internal Action
ESSA Program Planning: Next-Year Application Preparation
Recommended: October through November. State application windows vary.

Most SEAs open their consolidated application or funding request windows for the next school year in late winter or spring. Districts that begin preparation in Q4 (reviewing current-year program outcomes, documenting needs assessment findings, and updating use-of-funds narratives) submit stronger applications and avoid last-minute compliance gaps. This is a recommended internal action; your SEA’s actual application window is the hard deadline to track.

What to prepare in Q4
  • Current-year outcome data for each funded program: what worked, what did not
  • Needs assessment documentation updated (required for Title I schoolwide programs)
  • Use-of-funds narratives reviewed for supplement-not-supplant compliance
  • Parent and family engagement plan reviewed and updated (Title I §1116)
  • Verify authorized representative designation is current in your SEA’s system
Source: ED Office of Elementary and Secondary Education, ed.gov · Check your SEA’s website for the exact application window each year
Internal Action
Annual AI Tool Governance Review
Recommended: October through November. Not a regulatory deadline.

No federal regulation currently requires an annual AI tool governance review. This is a recommended internal practice grounded in existing federal obligations: FERPA’s data governance requirements apply to any tool that accesses student records (20 U.S.C. §1232g); COPPA compliance obligations rest with the vendor, not the district, but districts must verify that each vendor’s data practices qualify for school-provided parental consent before deploying tools to students under 13; and IDEA’s confidentiality requirements apply to tools used in special education contexts (34 CFR §300.610–300.626). Q4 review ensures documentation is current before a new fiscal year of tool adoption begins.

What to review annually
  • Complete inventory of AI tools in use, both approved and unapproved
  • Confirm a signed Data Processing Agreement (DPA) exists for every tool that accesses student data
  • Verify governing board approval is documented for each AI tool adoption
  • Confirm vendor is still operational and compliant after recent edtech failures
  • For tools used with students under 13: confirm vendor terms of service prohibit commercial data use, behavioral advertising, third-party data sharing, and using student data to train or improve the model; school-provided COPPA consent is not valid if any of these conditions are not met
  • Output review policies current for all student-facing generative AI tools
  • Incident response plan confirmed to include an AI-specific protocol
Federal basis: FERPA, studentprivacy.ed.gov · 20 U.S.C. §1232g · COPPA: 15 U.S.C. §6501 · IDEA confidentiality: 34 CFR §300.610–300.626
Check Your State
Title II Part A: Professional Development Documentation
SEA sets reporting windows. Federal allowability standard is uniform.
Why “check your state”: Title II Part A expenditure reporting flows through each state’s consolidated reporting system on a schedule the SEA sets. The federal standard for what constitutes an allowable professional development expenditure is uniform; the reporting window and format are not.

ESSA §2103 requires that Title II Part A funds be used for “high-quality professional development” as defined in §8101(42). Activities must be sustained, intensive, and classroom-focused, not one-day workshops. Reviewers ask whether districts can document that each expenditure meets the ESSA definition and was not used to supplant state or local professional development funding. Q4 is when to audit documentation before year-end reporting.

What to have documented
  • For each Title II-funded activity: duration, content focus, and evidence of classroom application
  • Supplement-not-supplant documentation: were these activities funded locally before Title II?
  • Personnel paid with Title II funds: time and effort records current
  • Needs assessment documented: Title II activities should connect to identified teacher/leader needs
Source: ESSA §2103 · ESSA §8101(42) (definition of high-quality PD) · ED Title II Program, ed.gov · Check your SEA for reporting window
